Why the game is sometimes hard to reach

A plain-language summary of what we looked into, what we found, and what we plan to do about it.

Prepared 24 May 2026

The short version

The game and all of its saved data are healthy — no faults found. The website is online right now, and every page loads. The likely reason some people occasionally can't reach it is that one page (the high-scores leaderboard) is built fresh on every visit and is slow. Pages built this way can briefly "fall asleep" when traffic is quiet, and waking them up sometimes takes long enough to fail. We have a clear, low-risk fix.

Where we looked

Think of the app as having three separate parts. We inspected the records (the "logs") for each one.

The part we checkedIn everyday termsWhat we found
The data store The storeroom where every game and score is kept. Healthy ✓
Error monitoring A brand-new "smoke alarm" that reports problems the moment they happen. Active, quiet ✓
The website host The service that delivers each page to visitors. Online, but one slow page ⚠
Storeroom: zero errors in 24 hours Smoke alarm: no real alerts yet Leaderboard page: ~2.5 seconds to load

What we found

The storeroom is in perfect shape — every request to it succeeded, and looking up the top-10 scores takes a fraction of a millisecond. So the data is not the problem.

The website is online. Most pages are pre-made, so they appear almost instantly. But the high-scores leaderboard is rebuilt from scratch every single time someone opens it, and that consistently takes about 2.5 seconds — while every other page responds in well under half a second. The delay isn't the data (that part is instant); it's the act of assembling that one page on demand.

An everyday way to picture it

Imagine the game is a shop:

📦
The storeroom Fully stocked and well-organised. Staff find anything instantly. (This is working perfectly.)
📄
Most pages Pre-printed flyers by the door — handed out the instant you walk in.
✍️
The leaderboard Hand-written fresh for each visitor by a staff member who, when the shop is quiet, has gone to the back and needs a moment to return.
Why it's "sometimes," not "always": when very few people are visiting, the part of the system that builds that page goes to sleep to save resources. The next visitor has to wait for it to wake up. Usually that's just a slow load — but occasionally the wake-up takes long enough that the visitor gets an error instead of the page. Because it depends on the exact timing of visits, it happens unpredictably, which is why it's been so hard to catch.

Importantly, these hiccups don't register as "errors" in the traditional sense — the system isn't breaking, it's just being too slow in that one spot. That's exactly why earlier checks came back clean and the cause stayed hidden.

What we recommend

  1. Pre-make the leaderboard and refresh it automatically Instead of rebuilding it for every visitor, keep a ready-made copy and refresh it every 30 seconds. It becomes instant and can no longer "fall asleep" or time out. This is the main fix, and it's low-risk.
  2. Keep a longer record of front-door traffic Today the host only keeps about an hour of delivery records, so a hiccup is often gone before anyone can look. Extending this means the next occurrence is captured with evidence.
  3. Let the new monitoring keep watch The "smoke alarm" we just installed will now flag genuine faults the instant they occur — including a specific configuration slip we want to rule out. No action needed; it simply needs to run.

Bottom line: nothing is broken in the game or its data. The intermittent trouble points to one slow, on-demand page that can stall when traffic is light. The recommended fix is straightforward and removes that exposure.

A few terms, in plain English

Pre-made / pre-printed page: a page prepared in advance so it can be shown instantly, the same way to everyone.

Built on demand: a page assembled freshly each time someone asks for it — more flexible, but slower, and able to "fall asleep" when idle.

Falling asleep (cold start): to save money and energy, parts of the system that aren't being used pause themselves. The first visitor afterwards has to wait for them to wake up.

Error monitoring: a tool that automatically reports problems as they happen, so we don't have to wait for someone to notice and complain.
